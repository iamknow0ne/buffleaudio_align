Historical Concept Plan: Advanced Vocal Alignment and Groove Manipulation Plugin

Status: archived reference. This document captures the original broad product concept and research notes. The current implementation roadmap lives in ROADMAP.md, and build/dependency notes live in README.md and docs/build.md. Treat ARA, Rubber Band, Allosaurus/ML phoneme detection, and MIDI groove mode in this file as later-stage ideas, not as v0.2 implementation commitments.

Introduction

This project aims to develop an audio plugin that automatically aligns multiple vocal layers to a reference vocal (lead) and provides an innovative groove manipulation feature. The plugin will tighten the timing of backing vocals or doubles against a guide track (similar to Synchro Arts VocALign) and allow dynamic adjustments of vocal timing and emphasis via MIDI input. Key goals include:
	•	Vocal Alignment Engine: Use state-of-the-art DSP (and possibly AI) to match the timing of a “Dub” track to a “Guide” track, ensuring energy peaks and transients align. This will greatly speed up editing of double-tracked vocals or ADR (dialog replacement).
	•	Consonant/Vowel Groove Control: Introduce a novel MIDI-driven feature to manipulate the vocal groove by selectively removing or altering consonants and vowels. For example, backing vocals can automatically have their consonants trimmed or attenuated to prevent clutter, or vowels can be retriggered in rhythm via MIDI for creative effects.
	•	Real-Time vs Offline Processing: Investigate feasibility of real-time alignment (e.g., live performance) versus offline processing (e.g., studio mixing) and likely implement as an offline/ARA-enabled plugin for maximal quality.
	•	Prototyping and Development: Leverage JSFX (Reaper’s scripting) for rapid prototyping of DSP algorithms, then transition to a robust C++ VST/AU plugin (using JUCE or similar) for cross-DAW release. We will weigh development speed against performance and distribution needs.
	•	AI/ML vs DSP: Consider conventional DSP techniques (dynamic time warping, cross-correlation, time-stretch algorithms) versus modern AI/ML approaches (speech recognition aligners, neural networks) for both alignment and phoneme segmentation. Use proven methods initially, with ML as a potential enhancement if it adds value.

The following sections detail the technical approach, algorithms, development steps, and feasibility for each aspect of the project.

1. Vocal Alignment Engine

1.1 How Vocalign (Synchro Arts) Works – Alignment of Vocal Layers

VocALign by Synchro Arts is the industry-standard for automatic vocal alignment. It works by applying time stretching and compression to a Dub (secondary) audio signal so that its energy peaks and troughs line up in time with a Guide (reference) signal. In practice, VocALign:
	•	Analyzes Waveforms: Both Guide and Dub signals are analyzed (often via a spectral or energy envelope analysis) to create a time-varying energy representation. Transient points and syllabic energy patterns are identified in each.
	•	Determines Time-Warp Path: Using advanced pattern recognition and dynamic programming, it finds the optimal time-warping path that maps Dub’s features onto Guide’s timeline. This typically involves a variant of Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) or Viterbi algorithm to minimize timing differences over the whole sequence. The goal is to align similar phonetic events while allowing elastic scaling of time.
	•	Applies Time Stretching/Compression: The Dub is then retimed according to this warp path. A time-domain or frequency-domain time-stretch algorithm (with minimal pitch change) is used to stretch or squeeze segments of the Dub. VocALign reportedly combines DTW alignment with techniques like PSOLA (Pitch Synchronous Overlap Add) for high-quality, formant-correct time stretching. The output is a new Dub track that is in tight sync with the Guide (often to a very high correlation in time domain).
	•	Sync Points and Fine Tuning: Some versions (VocALign Ultra) expose controls like alignment tightness to let users adjust how rigidly the Dub is aligned to the Guide ￼. A lower tightness preserves some natural offsets, while higher tightness locks the Dub nearly exactly to the Guide. Users can also choose different alignment algorithms or presets optimized for dialogue vs. vocals, etc., to improve results.

Our plugin’s alignment engine will draw on these principles. We will implement a similar pipeline: feature extraction, time-warp path calculation, and audio re-synthesis. The known effectiveness of DTW-based alignment means we can achieve reliable results by following this well-trodden path.

1.2 Core Algorithms and DSP Techniques

To implement the alignment:
	•	Feature Extraction: We will first convert audio to a sequence of features that emphasize timing information. A simple approach is using an energy envelope or amplitude envelope (possibly band-passed to focus on midrange frequencies of speech). More advanced: use MFCCs or spectral features to better distinguish phonetic content. However, for pure timing alignment, a smoothed energy or per-band onset detection function may suffice. VocALign’s documentation implies it uses identical spectrum analyzers on both signals to get comparable patterns, likely something like an ERB spectrogram or filter bank. We might implement a bank of filters or FFT to get energy vs. time.
	•	Dynamic Time Warping: Using the feature sequences, we find the optimal alignment between Dub and Guide. DTW will handle stretching some parts and compressing others. We will incorporate constraints (e.g., monotonic time increase, slope limits to prevent extreme time compression/expansion beyond a limit). The DTW cost function can be simple (e.g., Euclidean distance between energy levels) or more complex (pattern matching of transients). The output is a time-warp path mapping Dub’s timeline to Guide’s timeline. This is essentially what VocALign calls the “time-warping path”.
	•	Note: We may also explore using cross-correlation to get an initial rough alignment (to sync starting points or global tempo difference) before applying DTW locally. Cross-correlation can find a constant offset or drift, then DTW fine-tunes the rest.
	•	Time Stretching and Compression: With the warp map, we must resample the Dub accordingly. Options:
	•	Implement a simple time-domain resampler (low complexity but might produce artifacts on vocals).
	•	Use PSOLA to preserve formants and quality when stretching voiced sections (split waveform at pitch period markers and overlap-add). This is complex to implement but known to yield good vocal results.
	•	Use an existing high-quality library like Rubber Band (C++ library) which provides independent time-stretch and pitch-shift control ￼. Rubber Band is GPL licensed (we might need a commercial license for a closed-source plugin), but it would save significant development time and ensure quality. It uses frequency-domain techniques and offers transient preservation modes, which could be ideal for vocals.
	•	Another alternative is Elastique by Zplane (used in many DAWs) but that is not open-source. Given budget constraints, we lean towards Rubber Band or a custom PSOLA if we want full control.
	•	Alignment Tightness Control: To allow users to adjust how strictly the Dub follows the Guide, we will incorporate a tightness parameter similar to VocALign Project 5. Technically, this could interpolate between the identity (no alignment) and full DTW warp path. For example, we could dampen the magnitude of time shifts or apply a smoothing factor to the warping path so that small differences are left alone (looser, more natural double-tracking feel) whereas large differences (likely phrasing errors) are corrected. This control addresses the “preserving differences” requirement (discussed more in section 2.3).

Algorithmic Trade-offs: The above approach (DTW + time-stretch) is a conventional DSP method that is deterministic and requires no training data. It’s well-proven and likely to match VocALign’s behavior. The main trade-off is computational load: DTW is quadratic in time complexity, but we can optimize by downsampling the feature envelope or using a multi-scale approach for long audio. The time-stretch step can be CPU intensive, but libraries like Rubber Band are optimized in C++ (and can leverage SIMD). Overall, on modern systems, aligning a vocal phrase of a few seconds should be near-instant in offline mode.

Reference: VocALign’s known use of dynamic programming and time stretching ensures aligned vocals without manual editing. We will implement equivalent techniques with careful attention to audio quality (minimizing artifacts).

1.3 Real-Time vs. Offline Alignment

We need to consider if the plugin will operate in real-time (e.g., during live tracking or performance) or only as a post-processing/editing tool (offline).

Offline (Studio Workflow): Most vocal alignment tasks happen after recording. Tools like VocALign or Celemony’s Melodyne (with its time align features) are used in the DAW as an editing step. Modern DAWs support ARA (Audio Random Access) for such plugins, meaning the plugin can access the entire audio clip without real-time feeding. Our plan is to implement offline alignment first, likely via an ARA integration or a capture/align workflow: the user selects a Guide and Dub region, presses “Align”, and the plugin processes the Dub into a new aligned output. This yields the highest accuracy because the algorithm can see the whole performance at once and compute the global optimal alignment path. It also avoids strict latency requirements.

Real-Time (Live or Tracking): Real-time alignment is more challenging but could be a unique selling point if achievable. It would mean as a vocalist sings along, their voice is automatically pulled towards the guide track’s timing on the fly. To attempt this, the plugin would need to operate with some latency buffer:
	•	One approach: accumulate a short window (say 1-2 seconds of audio), perform a quick alignment of that chunk, output it aligned with some delay. This introduces latency roughly equal to the window length. It might be acceptable in a live sound scenario (e.g., 50-100 ms might be ok for in-ear monitoring, but 1-2 s would not). There’s a trade-off between latency and alignment accuracy.
	•	Real-time also can’t revise past audio once played, so it would have to align incrementally. An algorithm like online DTW or a neural network that aligns as it streams would be needed. This is complex and may not guarantee the perfect alignment that offline processing can.

Plan: Given the complexity, we will prioritize offline alignment, which covers the primary use cases (music production and ADR). We will design the plugin as an ARA-enabled VST3/AU so that DAWs like Reaper, Logic, Studio One, etc., can use it seamlessly (similar to how VocALign integrates). Real-time mode can be explored later as an experimental feature, perhaps with the limitation of a fixed latency. If implemented, we might restrict it to aligning a live Dub to a prerecorded Guide in situations where a slight delay is tolerable (e.g., live vocal doubling with a track). The core alignment engine would be the same, but executed on rolling buffers.

Feasibility: Offline alignment is highly feasible with today’s computing power and known algorithms. Real-time alignment is risky and complex, likely requiring either a very efficient algorithm or a predictive ML model. We will treat it as a stretch goal, ensuring the offline functionality is solid first.

1.4 AI/ML-Based vs. Conventional DSP Methods

Our alignment engine as described uses conventional DSP (deterministic algorithms). Could AI or machine learning improve this? We consider a few angles:
	•	Neural Network Alignment: One could train a neural network to directly align or time-stretch one signal to match another. For instance, a sequence-to-sequence model or even a transformer that outputs a warping path given two audio sequences. This might be similar in spirit to how some speech recognition systems align audio to phonemes (forced alignment using HMMs or CTC networks). However, training such a model would require a large dataset of misaligned and aligned vocal pairs, which is not readily available. It would also be a “black box” – harder to predict or control. At this stage, using ML for the core alignment seems unnecessary, as DTW is reliable without training data.
	•	Hidden Markov Models (HMMs): In speech, HMMs have been used for alignment by modeling likely phoneme durations and transitions ￼. An HMM could align at the phoneme level by finding the best state (phoneme) sequence alignment between Guide and Dub. However, this again requires a model of the speech content. VocALign’s advantage is it’s content-agnostic – it just matches patterns, no need for an actual transcription. For our scope (matching the same performance sung twice), a phonetic alignment is overkill and error-prone if the singing isn’t clear.
	•	Forced Aligners (Speech ML): There are open tools like Montreal Forced Aligner (Kaldi-based) or Wav2Vec2 aligners that can align a transcript to audio with high accuracy. We could possibly repurpose such an aligner by giving it the Guide as the “transcript” for the Dub – essentially aligning Dub audio to Guide audio via phonetic recognition. However, mispronunciations or different lyrics between takes would confuse it. And integrating a large ML model (which might require GPU) into a real-time plugin is not practical.
	•	AI for Segmentation (Phoneme Detection): A more promising use of ML is in the groove manipulation feature (Section 2), where identifying consonants vs vowels can benefit from a speech model. For alignment itself, one ML-based enhancement could be a classifier to detect and protect certain sounds (like plosive consonants) so the algorithm doesn’t mistakenly warp them. But this can be handled with simpler rules too (detect short noise bursts and avoid stretching them).

Conclusion: We will implement the alignment with conventional DSP first, ensuring a solid baseline. AI/ML doesn’t currently offer a clear advantage for the core alignment problem of matching two very similar signals (the DSP method already achieves that accurately). ML introduces complexity and dependency on large frameworks. That said, we remain open to using ML in supporting roles (e.g., phoneme recognition for the MIDI feature, noise/voice activity detection to focus alignment on voiced parts, etc.). These can be integrated as offline analysis steps rather than in the core real-time processing loop.

By balancing DSP and AI, we aim for a solution that is both reliable and innovative, using ML where it meaningfully enhances capability (like smart segmentation), and DSP where it already excels (time alignment and stretching).

1.5 Open-Source Libraries and Frameworks for Alignment

To accelerate development, we will leverage existing libraries and frameworks wherever possible. Key resources include:
	•	JUCE (C++ Framework) – We plan to build the plugin with JUCE, the most widely used framework for audio plugins. JUCE will handle cross-platform plugin creation (VST3/AU/AAX), audio I/O, and the GUI. Its popularity ensures maintainability and it has facilities for audio processing callbacks, parameter management, and even an integrated ARA support in the latest versions (useful for offline integration).
	•	Rubber Band Library – As noted, Rubber Band is an open-source (GPL) time-stretch/pitch-shift library ￼. It could save us from writing our own stretcher. We must be mindful of the license: either make our plugin open-source or purchase a commercial license for Rubber Band. If budget/time allows, buying a license for a closed-source plugin might be worthwhile given Rubber Band’s audio quality. Alternatively, we can keep the time-stretch part in a separate open-source module to comply with GPL, or initially release as open-source to test the waters.
	•	DSP Libraries – For lower-level DSP, libraries like Intel IPP or Eigen could help with vector math for DTW. Also, Librosa (Python) can be used in prototyping to test our alignment on example signals before coding in C++. We might prototype the DTW using Librosa’s DTW functions to verify output.
	•	Audio File I/O – If not fully using JUCE for audio data handling, libsndfile or dr_wav could be used for reading/writing audio in tests. But likely JUCE’s classes (AudioBuffer, etc.) suffice.
	•	Allosaurus (Phoneme Recognition) – An open-source phoneme recognition toolkit that can output time-stamped phonemes from audio. This could be integrated (possibly offline, via a Python script or a C++ port) to get a detailed segmentation of the vocal for our MIDI groove feature. Allosaurus models are relatively lightweight and could be bundled. We will explore using it to label each segment as consonant or vowel, to automate the splitting. (More in section 2.1)
	•	Aeneas – A Python/C library for forced alignment of audio to text. While focused on audiobook alignment, its methods (MFCC alignment) might provide inspiration for aligning two audio streams or could be adapted. However, it likely assumes a known text, so perhaps not directly useful for audio-to-audio.
	•	Aurio/AudioAlign – The Aurio library (C#/.NET) and the AudioAlign tool are examples of research into synchronizing audio recordings. These suggest methods like fingerprinting or landmark-based alignment which might handle cases with differing audio (not our primary case). We can reference their approach (maybe onset matching, etc.) but since our use case is same content, DTW is simpler.
	•	WDL (Cockos) and JSFX integration – If we prototype in JSFX (see Section 3), Cockos WDL provides utilities to embed the EEL2 (JSFX) interpreter or compile JSFX to a plugin. There’s also ysfx (a VST that hosts JSFX scripts). In a pinch, we could run the JSFX code inside our plugin for quick testing. But for the final product, we’ll translate everything to C++ for efficiency.

By utilizing these resources, we minimize reinventing the wheel. We ensure our development time is focused on the unique logic (alignment, segmentation, MIDI control) rather than low-level DSP coding that others have perfected. Each library choice will be reviewed for license compatibility and performance.

Development Note: Early in development, we will set up a basic JUCE plugin project and integrate Rubber Band (if chosen) to verify we can process audio buffers through it. We will also write unit tests for the DTW routine using synthetic data (e.g., a stretched copy of a waveform should realign to the original with minimal error).

2. Groove Manipulation via MIDI and Phoneme Control

Beyond simply aligning vocals, we want to offer a creative feature: dynamically altering the vocal groove by removing or modifying certain sounds (consonants/vowels) based on MIDI input. This could be used to tighten backing vocals in a mix or to create stuttery, rhythmic vocal effects in electronic music.

2.1 MIDI-Driven Vocal Segmentation and Processing Techniques

To manipulate vocals at the level of consonants and vowels, we must first segment the vocal performance into those units (or something similar, e.g., phoneme or syllable segments). Then, MIDI input can be mapped to affect those segments (mute, trigger, time-shift, etc.).

Segmentation Approaches:
	•	Amplitude Envelope Thresholding: A simple heuristic is that vowels tend to carry the sustained energy of speech, while many consonants (especially plosives like p,t,k or fricatives s,f) are short transient sounds with lower amplitude. By setting a threshold on the amplitude envelope, we can guess that high-amplitude regions are vowels and low-amplitude, short regions are likely consonants. This is fast and language-agnostic. However, it’s imperfect: a loud consonant (like a shout “Hey!” the H is noise but loud) could be misclassified, and soft vowel fades might drop below threshold.
	•	Phoneme Recognition (AI): A more robust method is using a speech recognition model to get phoneme timings. For example, the Allosaurus library can output a sequence of phonemes with start/end times for a given audio. Each phoneme can then be labeled as vowel or consonant using a phonetic feature table (as shown in the StackOverflow example code) ￼. This would give precise boundaries for each sound. We would implement this analysis offline when the user loads a vocal into the plugin (it might take a few seconds for a phrase). The advantage is high accuracy in identifying each segment; the downside is the complexity of including an ML model and slight processing time. We may start with this approach for development/testing (since correctness is high) and then see if a simpler method suffices for real-time use.
	•	Hybrid / Other Methods: We can use acoustic features to detect likely consonant regions: high zero-crossing rate or high-frequency energy bursts often indicate unvoiced consonants, whereas vowels have strong low-frequency formant structure. A band-pass filter approach could separate the signal: e.g., low-frequency energy envelope vs high-frequency could help distinguish vowels vs consonants. Onset detectors could mark transient attacks (often consonant onsets). Another idea: use the difference between the original and a heavily smoothed version of the waveform to isolate transients.

MIDI Integration Techniques: Once the vocal is segmented:
	•	We can map segments to MIDI in a way similar to how slicing a loop works in samplers (e.g., FL Studio’s Slicex or Ableton’s Slice to MIDI). For instance, each vowel segment could be assigned to a MIDI note number in sequence. Pressing that key would play that vowel segment. This is one way to re-trigger vowels via MIDI, creating vocal chops. FL’s Slicex indeed can auto-chop vocals and map them to a keyboard – it doesn’t specifically isolate vowels, but often cuts at transients. Our tool could improve on this by ensuring chops are musical (around vowels).
	•	For backing vocal tightening, the use-case is different: we might not want to manually trigger slices, but rather automatically mute or lower the volume of certain segments (like consonants) in the backing vocals. We could use MIDI in a more abstract way – for example, a MIDI key could toggle whether consonants are kept or muted. However, this might as well be a plugin parameter (on/off or percentage). The “MIDI input” idea might be more relevant to creative re-triggering than just removal.
	•	Another interpretation: Use a MIDI pattern (rhythmic pattern) to modulate the vocal’s rhythm. For instance, feed a MIDI sequence of notes and have the plugin align the vocal’s syllables to those note onsets (like quantizing the vocal to a groove). This is quite advanced: essentially using MIDI as a timing template. We could attempt a secondary alignment mode where instead of aligning Dub to an audio Guide, we align the vocal to a series of time points given by MIDI. That would involve stretching or skipping content to hit those time marks. If the MIDI represents a beat pattern, the vocal could be made to follow that rhythm tightly. This might create unnatural results if overdone, but could be creatively interesting.

Given the phrase “dynamically removing or altering consonants and vowels based on MIDI input,” our plan is to support two main MIDI-driven features:
	1.	MIDI-Triggered Vocal Slicing (Creative Mode): The vocal is sliced (with an emphasis on isolating vowels). The slices are assigned to MIDI notes or triggered in order by an incoming MIDI stream. The user can “play” the vocal like an instrument, creating chops or repeats. We will implement this by buffering the audio slices and playing them back on note-on events. If no MIDI is input, the vocal can play normally; with MIDI, we either override playback or gate it. This mode is similar to using a sampler but built into the plugin for convenience. It takes inspiration from popular production techniques for vocal chops where producers slice vocals and play them via MIDI. We will ensure slices start at zero-crossings or have small crossfades to avoid clicks.
	2.	Consonant Control for Layered Vocals (Mixing Mode): In this mode, the plugin automatically suppresses consonants on background layers to tighten the mix. This doesn’t require continuous MIDI input – it can be automated or static. But we might allow a MIDI key switch to toggle how aggressive the consonant removal is, or use a MIDI CC to continuously control it. The core idea: once we know the time ranges of consonants vs vowels, we can apply an envelope to reduce the consonant volume (or replace them with silence) on the Dub track. This ensures the lead vocal’s consonants aren’t flammed by the backing. This technique is often done manually by editors (removing or lowering backing vocal consonants to avoid doubling those hard sounds). We will automate it. The amount of attenuation could be user-controlled (e.g., “Consonant Level %”). Possibly, MIDI velocity of a trigger note could set how much to attenuate in a live setting.

MIDI Technicals: The plugin will accept a MIDI input in the DAW. In creative mode, it will respond to Note On/Off events to play slices. In mixing mode, it might respond to a designated CC or note (or simply not require MIDI at all, just a built-in process). We’ll implement a simple state machine for slice triggering and ensure timing is sample-accurate (the MIDI events will align with the DAW timeline so we can schedule audio frames accordingly).

Timing & Sync: If using MIDI to re-trigger audio, we have to consider how it syncs with the DAW timeline. Usually, in Ableton or FL, sliced vocal one-shots are just triggered by MIDI clips quantized to the grid. Our plugin should respect the DAW’s tempo. If the vocal is originally recorded at a certain tempo but we are re-triggering slices, essentially we are remixing its rhythm. There is potential to combine this with time-stretch: for example, hold a MIDI note to sustain a vowel longer than original (loop it or stretch it), or truncate it by releasing the key. These are advanced sampler-like behaviors (maybe out of scope for initial release). For now, we will implement one-shot triggering of the slice (play from start to end of slice when triggered, possibly with a choice to stop at slice end or continue into next slice if held).

Feasibility: The slicing and MIDI triggering are straightforward and similar to known sampler workflows. The challenge is automatically finding good slice points (hence the emphasis on phoneme detection). Another challenge is latency: when a MIDI note is pressed, our plugin should respond immediately (within a few milliseconds) to play the audio. This is doable if the audio is pre-loaded into memory buffers. We will allocate buffers for each slice on analysis. Memory usage for one vocal track of a few seconds is negligible by modern standards.

We must also consider edge cases: what if MIDI triggers slices out of original order? (We can allow it for creative freedom, like jumbling the syllables.) What if two notes are pressed rapidly? (We can allow overlap or cut the previous slice depending on desired effect – could make this a setting). Essentially, we’re building a rudimentary vocal sampler into the alignment plugin.

2.2 Existing Software with Similar Features

To gauge the novelty and get implementation hints, we researched existing solutions that touch on these ideas:
	•	Synchro Arts Revoice Pro: This is a more advanced sibling of VocALign which also adjusts pitch and allows some manual tweaking. Revoice can tighten multiple tracks and has a “Protect” feature to avoid modifying certain parts (like breaths or consonants) to keep things natural. While not MIDI-driven, it shows the importance of handling consonants carefully. It likely uses side-chains or internal logic to not align segments below a length threshold (implying they treat very short sounds specially, which we plan to do).
	•	Melodyne 5: Melodyne allows manual time stretching and alignment of vocals (and even has an automatic vocal alignment for multi-tracks). It doesn’t automatically remove consonants, but users often manually separate and adjust them. Melodyne’s “Levelling tool” can reduce the amplitude of selected notes (so one could manually lower consonants). Again, not MIDI-driven, but a reference for high-quality time editing.
	•	DAW Slice to MIDI (Ableton, FL Studio): As mentioned, slicing a vocal to a sampler is a common technique for EDM vocal chops. Ableton’s feature slices either on transients or at a fixed beat grid, then maps slices to a Drum Rack (playable via MIDI). FL Studio’s Slicex can auto-detect slice points (transient detection) and you can play the pieces. These tools do not distinguish vowels vs consonants, but they provide the creative triggering we want. We take inspiration but add the phonetic awareness to specifically target the linguistic elements of the vocal.
	•	Vocoders and Vocal Synths: Some vocal effects use MIDI to impose a melody onto speech (e.g., using vocoder or Auto-Tune in “Graphical Mode” with MIDI input to guide pitch). While not directly about timing, it’s relevant that MIDI can control aspects of a voice’s output. For instance, one user sought to slice live vocals via MIDI for glitchy effects. The consensus was to use tools like Autotune’s MIDI control or vocoders, but those alter pitch more than timing. Our plugin could fill this gap by altering timing instead, giving a new kind of vocal instrument.
	•	Manual Editing Techniques: In professional mixing, it’s common to edit backing vocals so that only one track (usually the lead) carries the strong consonants. Engineers will delete or soften consonants on background layers, so the words still sound in unison but without a flammed “ss” or “t” sound when multiple voices hit it slightly offset. This is time-consuming by hand; our plugin can automate it, which could attract users for the time saved and consistency achieved. There isn’t a known plugin that just does “consonant muting”, aside from using a de-esser or gate which is not selective enough (they act on frequencies or overall level, not specifically identifying phonemes). So, this feature would be relatively unique.
	•	Groove Quantize for Vocals: Some DAWs have “groove quantize” which nudges audio clips to a groove template (often used for drums). Applying that to continuous vocal audio is tricky (would require warping the audio to markers). Our MIDI groove idea parallels this concept – the MIDI acts like a groove template. We didn’t find an existing one-click solution for groove quantizing vocals; typically one would either manually warp in the DAW or just re-time the performance. So if we implement this nicely, it’s an innovation.

In summary, pieces of our idea exist in different domains (sampler slicing, alignment, mixing tricks), but no single tool offers an integrated solution. This project could be first-to-market with a MIDI-driven consonant/vowel editing capability, filling a niche for both corrective and creative vocal processing.

2.3 Preserving Layer Differences While Maintaining Cohesion

A potential pitfall of aligning and editing vocal layers is ending up with a result that’s too perfect, losing the natural variance that gives a chorus of voices its richness. We want to maintain cohesion (tight timing, intelligibility) but also preserve some differences (slight timing, timbral, or pitch variations that make each take unique). Here’s how we plan to balance that:
	•	Adjustable Alignment Tightness: As described in 1.2, we will provide an alignment tightness control. By not forcing 100% alignment, the user can allow small deviations. For example, at 90% tightness the plugin might leave tiny timing differences (a few milliseconds here and there) uncorrected, which can keep the “chorusing” effect of multiple takes. This prevents a robotic or flanged sound that can occur if two tracks are nearly sample-accurate aligned. Tightness can be a global setting or even automated over time (perhaps looser in a chorus for a big sound, tighter in a verse for clarity). This directly maintains some differences intentionally.
	•	Consonant Treatment: While we will often be removing redundant consonants on backing vocals, we might choose to preserve a very soft version of them rather than full deletion, so that the backing voice doesn’t sound unnaturally vowel-only. This could be done by leaving, say, 20% of the consonant level (or a short fade of it) instead of a hard mute. The result is the consonant is mainly heard from the lead, but the backing still has a subtle articulation that adds realism. We’ll allow the user to tweak how much of the backing consonants remain.
	•	Formant and Timbre Differences: When time-stretching, we need to ensure we don’t alter formants/pitch unless desired. Our alignment will strictly work in time domain. If using Rubber Band or PSOLA correctly, the formant character of each singer remains intact. This means one voice might sound slightly brighter/darker than another – which is natural. We won’t attempt to homogenize the vocal tone (that is out of scope, and usually not wanted except for doubling one person’s voice, which inherently has similar tone). If anything, we can add an option for formant shifting to match voices (like if one singer’s formants are different and the user wants them closer), but that’s a complex feature for later (VocALign itself doesn’t do this; Synchro Arts has a separate product Revoice that handles pitch/formant adjustments). So by default, timbral differences are preserved.
	•	Micro-Timing Variations: Apart from the tightness control, we could introduce an auto humanize function: after alignment, nudge each phrase of the Dub by a tiny random amount (a few milliseconds) or based on some rule, to avoid perfect lockstep. This might mimic how two singers, even when perfectly in time, still have minuscule differences. However, this might not be necessary if our alignment is not hyper-sample-accurate to begin with. We’ll test this by ear; if the aligned result sounds too sterile, we’ll implement a micro-delay randomizer as an option.
	•	Stereo Spread and Differences: Although not directly asked, when people double-track, they often pan voices left/right to create width. Our plugin could possibly include a feature to subtly vary the timing or phase between channels for a stereo spread. For example, if the user has two doubles, align both to the guide but then perhaps delay one by a few milliseconds relative to the other to get a wider image. This again adds difference while staying cohesive. This is more a mixing suggestion; we might document it rather than automate it unless time permits adding a “stereo spread” parameter.
	•	Evaluation: To ensure we maintain a good balance, during testing we will A/B compare the fully aligned version to the original and to a manual alignment by an engineer, checking that our output still sounds like two separate takes (just tightly synced) and not like one doubled mono track. We can also solicit feedback from beta testers (some producers) on whether the backing vocals still feel alive. The subjective element is important, so user control is key. Each project might need a different amount of alignment vs. natural looseness.

In practice, cohesion is achieved by aligning important points (onsets of words, emphases) so the rhythm/groove is intact across layers, while differences are preserved by not aligning everything else perfectly and keeping natural variations in delivery. Our tool will allow the engineer to decide that balance.

2.4 Technical Implementation Steps for Groove Feature

To implement the consonant/vowel MIDI-driven groove manipulation, we will follow these development steps:
	1.	Phoneme Segmentation Module: Develop a module (could be C++ or Python script integrated) that takes a vocal track and outputs a list of segments with type labels (vowel or consonant, or more detailed phoneme classes). We will start by testing Allosaurus or another phoneme recognizer on sample vocals to get timings. If that pipeline is complex, we’ll implement a simpler energy-based segmentation as a first pass. We will compare the results to ensure our simpler method is acceptable or identify where it fails. This step will produce data like: [ (0.0s-0.1s: consonant), (0.1s-0.5s: vowel), ... ].
	2.	Slice and Buffer Audio: Based on the detected boundaries, slice the Dub audio into an array of audio segments. In the plugin, we will allocate buffers for each segment for quick access. Add small crossfades at boundaries to avoid clicks. Label slices as needing special handling (if consonant segments are to be dropped in backing vocals, mark those).
	3.	Consonant Removal Processing: Implement a routine that processes the Dub audio buffer and attenuates or silences consonant segments. This can be done by multiplying those buffer sections by a gain (0 for full removal, or e.g. 0.2 for 20% level). The output of this can be auditioned to ensure it still sounds natural. This feature can be tied to a parameter “Consonant Level” 0-100%. No MIDI is needed for this static operation, but we will allow a MIDI event (like sustain pedal or a certain note) to toggle it on/off for convenience if needed.
	4.	MIDI Trigger System: Create a system to handle incoming MIDI in the plugin. In JUCE, the processBlock function gives MIDI buffer along with audio. We’ll parse note on/off events. We define a mapping from MIDI notes to our audio slices (e.g., C3 triggers slice 1, C#3 slice 2, etc., or some configurable root note). We also consider using the timing of MIDI: if notes come in sequence, we play slices sequentially; if a note is repeated, maybe retrigger same slice. We should decide if we allow arbitrary ordering (likely yes, for creative freedom).
	5.	Playback Engine: When a MIDI note on is received, schedule playback of the corresponding slice: copy the slice’s samples into the output buffer at the appropriate time. If the note occurs at a time not aligned to our processing buffer, we handle it with minimal latency. Possibly maintain an internal playhead for the vocal that advances either normally or jumps based on MIDI. This part requires careful handling to avoid losing sync. One approach: run the aligned Dub normally in sync with lead (for alignment purposes), but multiply its output by a gate that is opened only when MIDI says to. If no MIDI notes, we silence everything (in creative mode), meaning the vocal won’t be heard unless triggered. This effectively treats the vocal as a phrase sampler. Alternatively, we entirely control playback via MIDI (like a one-shot sampler independent of timeline). We’ll likely implement the latter for simplicity in creative mode. In mixing mode, the playback just follows the original timeline (just aligned and with consonants possibly reduced).
	6.	MIDI Timing Manipulation: (Optional/Advanced) Implement the “align to MIDI groove” feature. This would mean taking a series of MIDI note timestamps (for instance, all note-ons of a certain key) as the target timing for vowel segments. We would then create a warp map similar to alignment, but instead of matching another audio’s features, we match each vowel onset to a MIDI note time. In essence, quantize the vowel segment start times to the nearest/desired MIDI note. This can be done by stretching the silence or preceding consonant gap between vowels. It’s a bit complex to automate but could be done: e.g., if a vowel originally starts at 1.00s but MIDI note is at 0.95s, slightly speed up preceding audio to make it land earlier; if MIDI is at 1.10s, stretch some silence to delay it. We need to ensure it doesn’t sound off. For initial release, we might skip full automation of this and let the user achieve similar result by manually playing the slices via MIDI or adjusting the alignment manually. However, we’ll keep it in mind as a potential feature: a “Quantize to MIDI” button that uses the warp engine to align to a MIDI file’s notes. Feasibility of this will be revisited after core features are done.
	7.	User Interface & Controls: Design the UI to allow users to engage these features easily. For example:
	•	A toggle or tab for “Align Mode” vs “Groove Mode”. Align Mode shows controls for alignment (tightness, etc.), Groove Mode shows controls for slicing, MIDI, and consonant level. They can be used sequentially (align first, then groove process).
	•	Display the waveform of the Dub with markers where slices are. Possibly allow the user to adjust these markers if needed (stretch goals for UI).
	•	Provide a MIDI learn or root note selector for the slicing feature.
	•	Consonant level knob. Possibly a “solo consonants” audition button to hear what’s being removed (to build confidence it’s doing the right thing).
	•	Tooltips or help explaining that MIDI keyboard can trigger slices when in slice mode.
	8.	Testing on Examples: Use a variety of vocal recordings: some lead+double pairs from different genres (rap with fast consonants, long held choir notes, spoken word). Evaluate the alignment quality (should be tight), and then test the groove features: Does consonant removal indeed clean up the mix without artifacts? Can we play a vocal like an instrument and does it sound musically interesting? We will also test extreme cases (what if the user triggers slices out of order – does it glitch or handle smoothly). Optimize accordingly (e.g., add a short decay on slice end to avoid abrupt cut, etc.).
	9.	Optimization: Ensure that the segmentation and any ML components do not hog resources. If needed, we’ll perform heavy analysis only on user action (like hitting “Analyze” button or when audio is loaded) rather than every playback. The real-time CPU usage during playback with MIDI should be minimal – basically just reading from buffers, which is trivial for modern CPUs. The alignment DTW and analysis can be done in a background thread when the user initiates alignment, to avoid UI freeze.
	10.	Feasibility Check: At this point, re-assess each feature. Alignment is done – likely fine. The segmentation + MIDI part – if it proves too unreliable (say the segmentation fails on certain voices), we might implement a manual override where the user can adjust or add slice markers (maybe not graphically if UI time is short, but perhaps by ear using some “nudge” controls). Since segmentation is crucial, we’ll invest time to get it right, possibly training a small model ourselves if needed (e.g., using an existing dataset to refine vowel detection). We expect with existing tools, it’s feasible enough for at least common cases (most singing has clear vowel peaks).

By breaking it down this way, we ensure a structured development of this complex feature. Starting with segmentation and simple consonant removal is a must-have for mixing benefits; adding MIDI triggers is a more experimental creative tool but one that will set our plugin apart.

3. Development Roadmap and Feasibility

Finally, we outline the overall project timeline with milestones, incorporating the above technical plans:

Phase 1: Research & Prototyping (Weeks 1-3)
	•	Gather and study literature on vocal alignment and DSP (completed as part of this document). We now have clarity that DTW + time-stretch is the approach, and tools like Rubber Band can be used. We also have identified methods for segmentation (Allosaurus, etc.).
	•	Prototype alignment algorithm in a high-level environment: possibly Python with Librosa or MATLAB. Feed in a guide and a deliberately time-shifted dub, verify DTW can realign it correctly. This is to validate our understanding and tune DTW parameters (window sizes, feature type).
	•	Experiment with JSFX (Reaper) for a simple case: Use JSFX to read two audio files and apply cross-correlation or DTW to align one to the other. This tests real-time feasibility in a DAW environment. JSFX is convenient here because we can quickly script something and use Reaper’s performance meter. If JSFX proves too slow for large DTW, that’s fine (we expect to move to C++), but it may still be useful for testing small snippets or proving the concept sound-wise.
	•	Prototype phoneme detection: Write a Python script using Allosaurus to label a test vocal, verify that we can separate consonant vs vowel segments accurately (compare with manual annotation). If needed, adjust threshold or filtering approach for simpler implementation.

Phase 2: Core Alignment Engine Development (Weeks 4-6)
	•	Set up a JUCE project for the plugin. Implement basic audio passthrough to ensure plugin skeleton works in a DAW.
	•	Code the DTW alignment in C++: likely in a separate class that can take two arrays of feature values and output a mapping. Test this with unit tests (e.g., known sequences). Optimize using dynamic programming techniques (use float for cost, etc.).
	•	Integrate Rubber Band (or implement a basic time-stretcher as placeholder). If Rubber Band: compile and link it, verify we can call its API to stretch a test audio by a factor. If custom: implement a simple WSOLA or PSOLA for initial testing (Rubber Band is preferred to save time).
	•	Implement the alignment process: given two audio buffers (Guide, Dub), extract features, run DTW, then use RubberBand to stretch the Dub according to DTW map. There’s some complexity in applying the variable stretch – one method: derive a time vs. time function from DTW path, resample Dub by that mapping (could be done by splitting Dub into many small pieces and time-stretch each to fit intervals between guide markers). RubberBand might have an API to stretch to a new length; we may need to apply piecewise or use it in “tempo map” mode if available. We’ll explore its documentation.
	•	Output the aligned Dub and compare with the guide visually and by ear. Iterate until the alignment is tight and artifacts minimal.
	•	At this point, we have the core engine functional offline (triggered perhaps by a button in the plugin UI that we built for testing).

Phase 3: Basic Plugin Functionality (Week 7)
	•	Develop the UI for loading/capturing audio into the plugin. Ideally, leverage ARA: with ARA, the user wouldn’t need to manually record audio into the plugin; the DAW would provide the audio events. If JUCE supports ARA easily, integrate it. Otherwise, implement a manual capture (sidechain input for Guide, main input for Dub, user hits “Capture” to store them internally for processing). This is a bit clunky but workable in any DAW.
	•	Provide a button “Align” that triggers our engine. After alignment, we need to play back the aligned audio. We can either output it immediately (if the plugin is inserted on the Dub track, it can replace the audio with aligned audio in real-time). Or in a more offline sense, render the aligned audio to a buffer and then allow user to export it. VocALign typically places the aligned audio back in the DAW timeline via ARA or by printing to a new track. For our initial implementation, simplest is: plugin on Dub track, it reads Guide from sidechain, computes aligned version, and then during playback it mutes original Dub and plays the aligned version from memory. We’ll implement that.
	•	Add the alignment parameters to UI (tightness, maybe “max stretch allowed” etc.). Also add an on/off to bypass alignment if needed for comparison.
	•	Test in multiple DAWs for compatibility (Reaper, Ableton, Logic via AU, etc.), ensure sidechain or ARA works.

Phase 4: Groove Feature Implementation (Weeks 8-10)
	•	Integrate the phoneme segmentation into the plugin. Perhaps use a lightweight method first (silence detection, threshold) to avoid dependency issues. We might have to include a small DNN model if using Allosaurus; we’ll see if that can run in C++ (maybe via ONNX runtime or so). If too heavy, we’ll stick to DSP segmentation.
	•	Once segments are identified, implement the two modes: Mixing mode (attenuate consonants) and Creative mode (MIDI slicing).
	•	Mixing mode: connect the consonant attenuation to a knob and test with real backing vocals – does it improve clarity? Adjust algorithm (maybe add a slight fade out before and after consonant to smooth transitions).
	•	Creative mode: set up MIDI handling in JUCE (straightforward using AudioProcessor::processBlock). Map slices to MIDI notes. Write logic to handle note on: we’ll likely maintain an index of the slice and a playback position. Possibly treat each slice as a small audio file that can be triggered. JUCE can play back audio by copying it to output in small chunks each block or by using its sampler voice classes (JUCE has a Synthesiser class we could use to manage voices for each slice triggered, which might simplify voice stealing, polyphony, etc.). We might utilize that to avoid writing a lot of playback scheduling code from scratch.
	•	Allow user to toggle between normal playback and MIDI-triggered playback. In normal playback, slices are ignored (just continuous audio), in MIDI mode, original playback is suppressed and only MIDI triggers cause output.
	•	Provide visual feedback in UI: perhaps highlight which slice is being played when a MIDI note is pressed (useful for user to know the mapping). If possible, allow drag export of slices or MIDI drag (like some samplers do) – but that might be beyond our timeframe.
	•	Extensive testing of this mode: try playing a melody on the MIDI keyboard and see if the words make sense or at least sound cool. Try fast repeated notes for stutter effects. Ensure no crashes or buffer overruns when spamming MIDI. Fine-tune slice boundaries if needed (maybe always include the consonant right before a vowel with the vowel slice, so it sounds natural when triggered – e.g., include the “t” with the “a” in “ta” so that if you trigger the vowel slice, you actually hear the full syllable; this could be an option).

Phase 5: Polishing and Optimization (Weeks 11-12)
	•	Performance: Profile the plugin CPU usage. The alignment calculation happens on demand (not continuously), so that’s fine. The real-time playback, even with MIDI, should be low CPU (just reading from memory). The segmentation analysis might be heavier (especially if ML-based); ensure it’s done outside the audio thread (e.g., when user hits “Analyze” or “Align”, do it in a background thread and then signal completion). Memory: storing a few seconds of audio is fine; if user tries to align extremely long takes (like 5-minute vocals), memory usage could be high if we store entire aligned audio in memory. We might then stream from disk or ask them to do it piecewise. But typical usage is a line or a verse at a time.
	•	Error Handling: Add fallbacks if alignment fails (e.g., if guide or dub are silent or too short). If segmentation finds no consonants (maybe the track is ooohs with only vowels), handle gracefully. Ensure MIDI outside expected range is ignored or wraps around if we allow that.
	•	User Testing & Feedback: If possible, give a beta to a few testers (producers) to try on their sessions. Their feedback will be crucial on the UX and subtle audio quality aspects. They might point out if the aligned audio phasing sounds off or if the consonant removal makes backing vocals sound lisped, etc. We then refine.
	•	Documentation: Prepare a user manual explaining how to use the plugin, with examples: e.g., “to tighten doubles, insert plugin, set guide track, press align, adjust tightness; to clean up backing, enable consonant attenuate; to create vocal chops, switch to MIDI mode and play slices on a MIDI track.”
	•	Feasibility Summary:
	•	The vocal alignment feature is very feasible – it’s based on known algorithms and the main risk (audio quality of time-stretch) is mitigated by using a proven library. We expect to achieve results on par with VocALign in most cases. The feasibility is high and this is the core guaranteed deliverable of the project.
	•	The MIDI groove manipulation is innovative but also feasible in parts: segmentation can be done with existing tech, and slicing playback via MIDI is essentially building a sampler, which is standard. The risk lies in usability: will users find it useful and intuitive? We must ensure it integrates well (possibly users might need to print the MIDI-triggered performance to a new track for further editing – we should allow that easily, maybe by recording the plugin’s output). Another risk is the complexity of combining this with alignment – but logically, we perform alignment first (get a nicely aligned double), then do segmentation on that aligned track for further tweaks. This sequential approach should avoid conflicts.
	•	Using MIDI to quantize timing automatically (without manual triggering) is the most uncertain part; if time is short, we will postpone that sub-feature. The manual MIDI triggering covers a lot of ground already.

Phase 6: Release (Week 13+)
	•	By this point we plan to have a stable version. We will package the plugin for Windows and Mac (and possibly Linux if using JUCE easily allows it). We’ll also consider what license to release under and handle any open-source attributions (if we used GPL code and decide to open source the plugin, that decision will be made here; or ensure we have a license for Rubber Band if closed-source).
	•	Market the plugin highlighting: “Tighten your vocals with sample-accurate alignment in seconds – no more manual nudging. Plus, inject new groove: auto-mute backing consonants for clarity or play your vocals via MIDI for creative chops and stutters.” This dual angle should attract both engineers and producers.
	•	Set up a feedback channel for post-release improvements (like if users want support for aligning polyphonic signals or multi-dub alignment simultaneously, etc., those could be future updates).

By following this roadmap, we address each technical challenge methodically. The plan is ambitious but broken into clear components. Each feature’s feasibility has been considered with fallbacks (e.g., simpler segmentation if ML fails, focusing on offline if real-time is too hard). We expect the core alignment to be delivered as a solid product, with the MIDI groove features as the “wow” factor that, if executed well, will differentiate our plugin in the market.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this project will combine conventional DSP algorithms (dynamic time warping for alignment, time-stretching) with modern techniques (possible AI phoneme recognition, advanced DAW integration via ARA) to create a powerful vocal production tool. The trade-offs between rapid prototyping in JSFX vs. building a full-fledged VST have been weighed, with a plan to leverage JSFX for quick tests but JUCE/C++ for the release to ensure performance and portability. Real-time operation is considered but will likely be limited, focusing instead on high-quality offline processing as used in professional workflows. We will utilize open-source frameworks like Rubber Band and possibly Allosaurus to stand on the shoulders of existing tech, accelerating development.

The innovative MIDI-driven groove manipulation feature will allow producers to reshape vocal rhythms in ways previously only possible with tedious editing or creative sampling. By automatically segmenting vocals into vowels and consonants, we can both clean up vocal stacks (for a polished, tight mix) and open up new creative avenues (playing vocals on a keyboard for remixing). This dual capability – precision alignment and groove creativity – is the core vision of the plugin.

Throughout development, careful attention will be paid to preserving the musical feel of performances. The aim is not to destroy the human quality of vocals, but to enhance and align it, or selectively modify it in musically interesting ways. With user-adjustable parameters for tightness and segment emphasis, the tool will be flexible for different genres and preferences.

By following the detailed plan above, we anticipate a successful implementation. Each component is grounded in proven research or practice, and our integration of them will be the primary engineering effort. The result will be a comprehensive vocal alignment and processing plugin that addresses real needs in music production and post-production, potentially saving hours of work and enabling novel sound design techniques.

References: The plan builds on known methods from Synchro Arts VocALign’s alignment approach, leverages frameworks like JUCE for deployment, acknowledges performance considerations of JSFX vs native code, and incorporates research insights such as combining DTW with PSOLA for vocal alignment. The consonant/vowel idea is informed by speech processing techniques and common studio practices. These references and findings ensure the project is rooted in solid audio engineering knowledge while pushing into new territory by combining these elements into one coherent tool.
